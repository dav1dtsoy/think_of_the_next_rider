Bicycle parking guide

Put yourself in someone else's pedals

A short guide to parking your bicycle thoughtfully at public spots.

scroll

Six steps · public bicycle parking

1

Find a designated rack

Look for official bike racks — the U-shaped, wave, or post-and-ring kind provided by the city or venue. Avoid locking to lampposts, benches, railings, or private fences unless no rack is nearby. Improvised anchors can obstruct pedestrians and may result in your bike being removed by authorities.

2

Approach and position carefully

Wheel your bike alongside the rack and align it in the intended direction — usually perpendicular. Keep your handlebars turned inward so the bike sits flush without jutting into the path of other cyclists or pedestrians approaching the rack.

Tip: On a crowded rack, stagger your bike into the next slot and alternate which side faces the rack. It allows more bikes to share the same space.

3

Lock the frame, not just the wheel

Pass your lock through the main frame triangle and around a fixed part of the rack. A wheel-only lock leaves the entire frame vulnerable — a thief can remove the locked wheel and walk away with the rest of the bicycle. When space is tight, always prioritize the frame.

4

Use a quality lock

A rigid U-lock or heavy-duty chain resists cutting tools far better than a cable lock, which can be severed in seconds. For extra security, use two locks of different types — this forces a thief to carry two separate tools, which is a strong deterrent in public spaces.

Tip: Position the lock with the keyhole facing downward. It makes it significantly harder to use leverage or pick tools on the cylinder.

5

Leave space for others

Once locked, check that your bike does not block other parked bikes, the rack access point, or the surrounding path. One inconsiderately placed bicycle can make an entire rack unusable for everyone else. Think of how you would feel arriving to find all slots blocked by a single bike angled across two spaces.

6

Take valuables with you

Detach your lights, remove saddlebags, and take any accessory that can be unclipped in under a minute — these are easy targets for opportunistic theft. A quick photo of your setup and the rack's location is useful if you ever need to file a report with the municipality.

Good parking is a gift to the next rider
